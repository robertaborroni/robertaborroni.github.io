SALAD

The Objective

What Really Drives
the Choice?

"Consumers are not buying a light salad — they are buying a functional, filling lunch solution."

A two-phase sequential research design combining qualitative exploration and quantitative validation to identify a promising product format for a new ready-to-eat salad line in the Italian market, uncovering the functional, emotional, and perceptual drivers behind purchase decisions.

🔬

Qualitative Phase

10 in-depth semi-structured interviews with Gen Z consumers aged 20–28 to explore the "why" behind consumption.

📊

Quantitative Phase

81 questionnaires (59 valid) via Google Forms, validating patterns identified in the exploratory phase.

🎯

Target Profile

Young urban adults 18–24 (52%), predominantly female (67%), students and full-time workers.

💡

Product Concept

Grilled chicken salad bowl, €5.00, transparent packaging, fork included, separate dressing.

Hypothesis Testing

Four Hypotheses.
All Accepted.

H1 — Freshness & Convenience
✓ Accepted

Both factors rated highly significant. Freshness (M=3.98, Mode=5) slightly stronger than convenience (M=3.49). Combine visible freshness + ready-to-eat format to maximise appeal.

H2 — Packaging
✓ Accepted

Bowl with fork (40% preference) + ingredient visibility (M=4.46) are the strongest drivers of purchase intention. Ingredient separation is secondary (M=3.10, widely dispersed opinions).

H3 — Price Barrier
✓ Accepted

Reasonable price: mean €4.34, mode €5. Too expensive threshold: mean €8.91. Price salads at €4–5 to maximise purchase intention; avoid pricing above €8.

H4 — Preferred Protein
✓ Accepted

Animal proteins dominate: grilled chicken 34%, cheese 18%, tuna 16%, eggs 12%. Plant-based total 19%. Zero respondents preferred no protein at all.

RICERCA

The Brief

How Well Do Young
Grads Know Market
Research?

"Il settore del marketing e, in particolare, quello delle ricerche di mercato, forse è ancora poco conosciuto."

ASSIRM — the Italian market research association — wanted to understand how well the sector is known among graduates in psychology, sociology, statistics, economics, marketing, and communication, and what actions to take to transmit the sector's value and professional opportunities to young people.

🎯

Objective 1

Measure how well the market research profession is known, valued, and considered by young graduates in related fields.

📣

Objective 2

Identify concrete actions ASSIRM can take as a sector association to communicate the value of market research to young graduates.

🔍

Area 1 & 2

Work perspective and professional sectors: how graduates imagine their professional future and what sectors they consider.

💼

Area 3 & 4

Market research as a discipline and as a career: specific knowledge and imagination of working within the sector.

Key Findings

What Emerged
from the Group.

01
🎨
Creativity Dominates

In both marketing and communication, the word "creativity" emerges repeatedly as the defining quality of work in these areas. Alongside it: attention to both verbal and non-verbal communication.

02
🌫️
Low Sector Awareness

Knowledge of market research — for those who don't work in it — is relatively scarce and comes almost exclusively from university, which often fails to spark genuine interest in the field.

03
👩‍💼
Prototypical Worker

Participants imagine a middle-aged woman who is precise, punctual, and detail-oriented — critically curious, with a flexible educational background depending on the type of research.

04
🎤
Open to Events

Participants are willing to attend in-person events and free online webinars to learn more about the sector — curious, but needing a bridge from theory to the lived reality of the profession.

Recommendations for ASSIRM

Three Actions.
One Goal.

01
Events & Webinars

Organise in-person events and free online webinars featuring experienced professionals — to answer the questions graduates are genuinely curious about: "What do you like about this job? What are the recurring challenges?"

02
Bridge Theory & Practice

Close the gap between academic theory — which often distances graduates from the sector — and the practical reality of working in market research. Make the day-to-day visible and relatable.

03
Sector Storytelling

Give the sector a human face. Use storytelling from real practitioners across different research typologies to show how varied and intellectually stimulating the work truly is.

SOLE

The Campaign

Protect Your
Skin. Own
Your Story.

"La combinazione di apprendimento vicario, imitazione ed educazione all'azione rappresenta un modello potente per modificare il comportamento."

A social marketing campaign targeting Italians aged 18–29, promoting sun protection habits, regular dermatological check-ups, and UV risk awareness. Grounded in Social Learning Theory: people learn by observing admired figures and responding to social pressure from peers — applied here through influencer strategy, viral video, and university workshops.

🎯

Sensibilizzazione

Educate young people on UV risks and promote primary prevention habits including daily SPF use.

🔄

Behavioural Change

Encourage regular sunscreen use, abandon tanning beds, and shift daily habits toward sun-safe behaviour.

🏥

Prevenzione Secondaria

Promote periodic dermatological check-ups for early diagnosis of skin cancer.

📲

Social Learning

Leverage vicarious learning (admired figures) and social pressure (peer inclusion) for lasting change.

Communication Strategy

Five Channels.
One Message.

01
📸
Instagram

Eleonora Petrella targets the female audience with skincare and sun protection advice. Max Angioni uses humour to reach the male audience on the importance of prevention. Posts and stories combining information with a light, engaging style.

02
🎬
TikTok

A viral street interview video: a short teaser hooks users, direct questions to passers-by reveal real habits, and the conclusion delivers a sensibilisation message with statistics and expert advice from dermatologists.

03
🎓
University Workshops

Interactive 20–30 minute talks by Fondazione Veronesi dermatologists covering UV damage and sun protection science. Followed by live Q&A sessions to deepen engagement with students.

04
🎁
Promo Kits

Distributed at workshops: high-quality sunscreen samples, an informative brochure co-signed by the influencers, and a QR code directly linking to the Fondazione Veronesi dermatology booking platform.

Has never considered sun protection a priority but is starting to show interest. Seeks simple information and affordable products compatible with a student budget. Responds well to humour-based messaging (Max Angioni) and peer-normalisation of SPF use.

Passionate about beauty and lifestyle, follows skincare trends on social media. Her choices are based more on generic suggestions than real skin knowledge. Curious to learn about prevention and dermatological visits. Responds to expert-backed, beauty-adjacent messaging (Eleonora Petrella).
